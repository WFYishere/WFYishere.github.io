1.3 Algorithm Analysis

Modern Computers can process around 10810^8108 problems

Familiarity with these bounds:

  • 210=1024≈1032^{10} = 1024 \approx 10^3210=1024≈103, 220=1,048,576≈1062^{20} = 1,048,576 \approx 10^6220=1,048,576≈106.
  • 10!=3,628,800≈3×10610! = 3,628,800 \approx 3 \times 10^610!=3,628,800≈3×106, 11!=39,916,800≈4×10711! = 39,916,800 \approx 4 \times 10^711!=39,916,800≈4×107.
  • 32-bit signed integers (int) and 64-bit signed integers (long long) have upper limits of
    231−1≈2×1092^{31} - 1 \approx 2 \times 10^9231−1≈2×109 and
    263−1≈9×10182^{63} - 1 \approx 9 \times 10^{18}263−1≈9×1018
  • If you need to store integers ≥264\ge 2^{64}≥264, use Big Integer.
  • The largest input size nnn for typical programming contest problems must be <1M< 1M<1M.
    Beyond that, the Input/Output (I/O) routine will be the bottleneck.
  • Usually, O(nlog⁡2n)O(n \log_2 n)O(nlog2​n) algorithms are sufficient to solve most contest problems for a simple reason:
    O(nlog⁡2n)O(n \log_2 n)O(nlog2​n) and the theoretically better O(n)O(n)O(n) algorithms are hard to differentiate empirically
