Thoughts for Attending Guest Lecture on Pi

Posted on 2024-02-20 Edited on 2024-02-22 In Thoughts , Experiences Word count in article: 3.2k Reading time ≈ 3 mins.

Attending a guest lecture is fun # Thoughts

I never thought about attending guest lectures before. Mainly because all the guest lectures that I attended in high school didn't talk much about "knowledge", but rather about "the history of knowledge" or the guests' experiences. Since we needed to compete for Olympiad and prepare for College Entrance Exams, we didn't have much time for doing "useless" stuff other than studying directly for the exams(at least, that's what I thought, I'll probably write about that as well in another post. If I have time). So I've always been kind of resistant to guest talks.

But then, I've started to change my mind since I came to university in a different country and tried to touch on things that I didn't want to take before(I'll also talk about that in the other post). Plus, I read Cal Newport's How To Win At College (I know this book sounds uitilitarian but it's actually good), in which he suggested listening to guest lectures twice a week. So I've decided to give it a try on a Maths Talks, since I want to know whether my interest actually lies in this realm.

The talk was given by prof Chan Heng Huat, from the Department of Mathematics in NUS. There were also several other profs who attended the meeting such as my prof from GEA1000N (What a surprise that I would be seeing him tmr) and profs from non-mathematics departments. Apart from this, several kids were also there in the talk, listening carefully and silently. That actually surprised me and I was even kind of jealous of those kids who get the chance to get exposed to these interesting talks when they were young(maybe one of them would be the next Ramanujan, who knows!).

Prof Chan mainly talked about the progress and various kinds of techniques or thoughts that were related to apporximating Pi. He used simple language, but dived deep into the thought progress of doing these approximations and gave useful details about the questions that were raised during the talk. The talk lasted for 40 minutes. It wasn't useful in a sense that I would probably forget about all these series and integrations next morning when I wake up. However, I would definetely say that it makes me remember why and how I loved Mathematics when I was young. I can't sum up now about what the talk brought to me or what I've learned in the talk, but I know in my heart that something was changed in my mind. Something was envoked. I cannot tell what it is right now, nor do I know how this would influence my future careers. I can't even say that it is "useful".

But as I am writing this, I remember David Brooks's talk about how everyone has this part of them that does not care about fame or money or tiktok likes, there is a part of us that pursues true happiness and higher realm. I guess I begin to remember how I like Maths as Maths itself, not because "Studying Maths Sounds So Hardcore" or "Maths Is Useful for CS" or anything like that. And I guess that's what guest lectures are for. To guide you to a higher perspective and envoke interest. To put down the utilitarian thoughts and discover something new. To truly enjoy the thing you love and remove the impurities imparted by reality to those things.

I look forward to attending more guest lectures.

This lecture is not a Special one. I wouldn't say that the lecture is the-thought-provoking-one. But as a first guest lecture that I take the initiative to participate, and as a first guest lecture that I attend in university, it does reminds me of something deep in my heart and inspires me to attend future guest lectures.
